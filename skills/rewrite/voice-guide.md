# The Voice — a style guide distilled from seven short stories

This is a portable, language-agnostic distillation of one author's prose voice, extracted from seven of their short stories (*Rzeź niewiniątka*, *Kula Borgesa*, *Cesarz*, *Czołem, przyjacielu*, *Hipotermia*, *Król żyraf*, *Pomyłka* — originally written in Polish). The rules below are stated for **English** prose. The `rewrite` skill loads this file and applies it.

Read **"How to apply this"** before anything else. The fastest way to wreck this voice is to apply all of it at once.

---

## The voice in one breath

Erudite and ironic, but never warm-blooded for long. A mind that thinks in paradox and antithesis, builds long accreting sentences that reason their way forward — and then snaps to a two-word line that lands like a blade. It lives on the em-dash. It collides high register against low (scholarly, liturgical, technical, bureaucratic vs. street, childlike, deadpan-modern) and mines the seam for irony. It is metafictionally self-aware: it names its own machinery, addresses the reader or an absent figure, drops dry asides in parentheses. It rations its imagery around one or two controlling symbols and detonates them at the close. It **understates** its deepest feeling — the cruelest act gets the plainest clause. It treats the cosmic and the absurd with the same straight face. And it ends on a short, lapidary line that reframes everything before it, often looping back to where it began. Melancholy runs underneath the wit the whole time.

---

## How to apply this (read this first)

1. **Restraint over flourish.** Every effect in these stories is *earned* and *rationed*. Fragments work because long sentences surround them. Images hit because there are few of them. If you crank every move to maximum, you get parody. When in doubt, do less.
2. **The voice is a range, not a costume.** It spans clinical detachment (*Czołem, przyjacielu*) to breathless panic (*Hipotermia*) to scholarly calm (*Kula Borgesa*). Find the register the draft is *already* reaching for, then sharpen that — don't impose a single mode on everything.
3. **Meaning is sacred.** You are re-voicing, not co-authoring. Do not invent plot, facts, characters, dialogue content, or events. Do not change the POV or tense unless they're broken or inconsistent. Preserve what the draft means and what the writer is trying to do.
4. **Match the source's reach.** A plain functional passage gets fluency + light rhythm, not metaphysics. A lyrical passage can take the full voice. Don't gild a grocery list.
5. **Tighten, don't pad.** Re-voicing usually *shortens*. If your rewrite is longer than the draft, you're probably adding instead of sharpening.

## The core fingerprint — eight moves that recur in every story

These are the highest-confidence rules. Consider them on every rewrite; deploy the ones the passage can carry.

### 1. The rhythm dial: build long, then detonate short
The signature move. Run a long, clause-stacked, semicolon-and-dash sentence that *thinks* — concession → correction → consequence — then cut hard to a very short sentence or a verbless fragment, often isolated as its own one-line paragraph. The contrast is the engine. The short line marks a turn, a shock, a verdict, or an emotional beat.

> He stepped closer, past the overturned stool and the dark pooling where the floor dipped, close enough now to see into the crib.
>
> And froze.

Never let short fragments pile up unearned — they only detonate against length.

### 2. The em-dash is the master joint
The dominant punctuation. Use it for: (a) a beat of held breath right before a reveal or the violent verb; (b) a mid-sentence pivot or reversal, in place of "but" or "and so," when you want the swing to feel sharper; (c) appositive expansion and accumulation; (d) a conversational narratorial aside; (e) interrupted or trailing-off dialogue. Prefer it over the semicolon for in-the-moment thinking, and over the colon for a dramatic swerve.

> Just dust — he was sure of it — until the dust developed an edge, and the edge developed intent.

### 3. Collide high register against low
Hold an elevated baseline (scholarly, archaic/liturgical, technical, legal-bureaucratic) and puncture it with the colloquial, the slangy, the childlike, the deadpan-modern — and vice versa. The clash *is* the humor and the irony. Don't homogenize a draft to one tone; sharpen the contrast between its registers.

> Space is a genuinely interesting place. A bit empty, but pleasant. And cold. And full of radiation. And, most of the time, boring.

### 4. Understate the peak
At the most violent or most painful moment, use the plainest, shortest syntax available. State the worst thing in one flat clause and refuse to dramatize it. Often follow with a single quietly ironic line that reframes — never consolation that comforts.

> On the third day he cut the boy's throat and left the village.
>
> At least he had saved his son.

Restraint, not amplification, carries grief and horror here. Cut adverbs and emotional signposting at the peak.

### 5. One controlling image, seeded and detonated — and rationed
Anchor the piece to a small number of image-families (often one master image: a mirror, a sphere, a coin-flip, a sea voyage, an empire, an author at work). Plant it early, re-angle it through different lenses, and let it pay off at the close. **Spend figurative language only where it advances that center** — keep the rest of the prose concrete and unornamented so the images that do appear hit hard. Reserve the starkest concrete image for the ending.

### 6. The ironic, self-aware narrator
Keep a dry, deadpan distance. Let the narrator know it's telling a story: name the genre machinery ("convention demanded it"), call an image what it is ("obvious symbolism"), admit the artifice, hedge its own certainty, address the reader as "you" or apostrophize an absent figure at thematic peaks. Park the wink in a short parenthetical — a second voice in the margin.

> He never received any power, that's obvious — but even so, he gained a few allies.

### 7. End on a short, reframing last line
Close on a brief, lapidary line that *recasts* what came before rather than summarizing it: a coined epithet, an ironic verdict, a diminished concrete image, a fused symbol, an isolated interior thought. Favor ring composition — answer the opening, snap the loop shut. End on the image, not on explanation.

> An emperor is an emperor. Even one who sleeps in stairwells.

### 8. Rule-of-three, anaphora, antithesis — and toggle conjunctions for tempo
Lean on triads (three short nouns or imperatives), balanced antithetical pairs ("We arose because we could. We will die because we must."), and anaphoric runs (successive clauses from the same opener) for incantatory build. Control speed with conjunctions: **strip them** (asyndeton) for urgency and clipped intensity; **pile them on** ("and… and… and") for ritual, compulsion, or an endless accumulating drift.

## Sentence architecture & rhythm

- **Default to the long, hypotactic sentence for thought and exposition** — subordinate and participial openers, a main clause, then a semicolon- or dash-linked second movement. Resolve late. Let one sentence carry a whole chain of reasoning so it reads like a mind working, not a list.
- **Break that rhythm deliberately** with the short detonation (move #1). The ratio matters: mostly long, occasionally very short.
- **Use fragments as terminal beats** — a lone noun phrase, a bare measurement — to simulate a hard cut or a freeze-frame. Strip the verb when the image alone should land.
- **For sudden action, chain bare verbs** with minimal connectors and no auxiliaries: "sprang back, seized the staff, drew both blades, charged." The piled predicates make speed.
- **For mounting tension or ritual, chain short clauses with repeated "and"**: "he loaded the tape, and rewound it, and pressed play."
- **To accelerate toward a collision, use a literal descending count** in progressively shorter lines: "Twenty metres. / Ten. / Faster now."
- **Periodic suspension:** front-load qualifiers and repeated appositive phrases, withhold the verb/payoff to the end so the sentence climaxes on its irony.

## Punctuation

- **Em-dash:** see core move #2. It's the fingerprint. Don't import the Polish leading-dash dialogue format — but keep the *interruption aesthetic* (lines that break off on a dash rather than finishing).
- **Semicolon:** join two clauses that are stages of one evolving thought, especially balanced or antithetical pairs ("death vs. politics"; "then… but increasingly…"). Higher density than typical English prose, in the contemplative/expository passages.
- **Colon:** a forward hinge — assert something compact, then let the colon open onto the proof, the list, the quoted artifact, or the bare-noun reveal. Acts as a "camera reveals:" operator.
- **Ellipsis:** reserve for a thought dissolving into doubt or trailing into the unsaid/infinite. Reinforce by repeating the last word once before the dots ("then… then…"). Distinguish from the dash: "…" *fades*, "—" is *severed* (use the hard mid-word dash-cut when something interrupts).
- **Parentheses:** the ironic/scholarly side-channel — a self-contained aside, a hedge, a wink, a darker counter-thought running beneath the confident main text. Keep them short and deadpan.
- **Italics:** systematically and sparingly — (a) vocal stress on a single pivotal word, (b) embedded quoted song/scripture/found-text, (c) foreign terms, (d) an isolated closing interior thought. Keep the uses distinct so emphasis never reads as decoration.

## Register & diction

- **Wear erudition lightly but unapologetically.** Let specialist vocabulary, real names, citations, and untranslated foreign tags (Latin especially) stand unglossed — but ration them to the "research/thought" register, never the action.
- **Coin aphorisms.** Periodically distill the theme into a short, balanced, proverb-like line with a concrete vehicle ("blood is like ink: it spills in every direction"). Keep them rare so each rings.
- **Drop speculative or technical detail flatly**, as already-normal furniture of the world — never spotlight it as a novelty.
- **Let one precise abstract noun name the theme** rather than spelling out the moral in a sentence ("the advancing *numbness* eating away at society"). Trust the reader.
- **Puncture with the colloquial:** an idiom, slang, a sarcastic vowel-stretch ("suuure"), chat-speak, a mild repeated oath used as a recurring beat. This keeps the high register honest and human.

## Imagery & metaphor

- **Build the central metaphor by inverting a familiar image's expected function** (a mirror that *builds a world* instead of reflecting one). Let the reversal carry the conceptual payload.
- **Run one image from metaphor to literal** across the piece — the abstract early use cashed out as physical fact later (lineage-as-spilling-ink → an actual pool of blood).
- **Keep similes short, concrete, physical**, and make each do thematic or characterizing work — draw them from the story's own image-world rather than at random. Zoomorphic/diminishing comparisons can enact a character's gaze.
- **Personify the antagonistic environment** with verbs of appetite and intent (it *devours*, it *sets traps*, it is *merciless*) — then undercut with a flat statement of indifference ("it simply is"). The oscillation is the theme.
- **Occasional compact personification** that fuses two facts of a setting into one surprising image — one sentence, then move on.

---

## Narrative voice & irony

- **Close third or first person, past tense, is home base** — but slip into the **historical present** for a "live" set-piece (memory, climax) and snap back; the tense-shift is a spotlight that says *watch this closely*.
- **Foreground unreliability when it's there:** let a narrator question the source of his own certainties ("How do I know? I always have") and admit when he's losing the thread. Turn it into an explicit, plot-driving feature, not a hidden one.
- **Affectionate mock-deflation of authority:** revere and puncture in the same breath ("Borges, that old charlatan, this time happened to tell the truth").
- **Free indirect discourse:** render a character's reasoning or doubt as unmarked rhetorical questions inside the narration, including mid-thought self-corrections set off by dashes.
- **Hide the moral.** Deliver social or emotional weight through word choice and a single ironic intrusion (often the last line), not through editorializing.

---

## Dialogue

- **Use English quotation marks** (not the Polish leading dash) — but keep the leanness it produced.
- **Minimal tags.** After establishing who speaks, drop tags and let short alternating lines carry the scene. When a tag appears, give it one job — a single manner word, or better, an **action beat** ("She shrugged. 'Half and half.'") that does the attribution and characterizes at once.
- **Stylize over naturalism when the piece is parabolic:** let characters speak in compressed, faintly aphoristic, slightly archaic lines that serve the theme. In gritty scenes, go fully colloquial and crude. Pick per scene.
- **Interrupt and overlap** in emotionally loaded exchanges — speakers talk over each other, lines snap off mid-clause, the gaps carry the subtext.
- **Repeat a short line verbatim** across several beats (with narration between) to build menace, intransigence, or ritual.
- **Concentrate dialogue in the one scene that must play in real time;** report or summarize speech elsewhere.

---

## Openings & closings

- **Open cold, in medias res, or on a confident flat assertion** that states the strangest fact as established record ("Alberto Aguilar proclaimed himself emperor when he turned forty-five") or a charged concrete contrast ("The boy was breathing fast. He wasn't breathing at all."). Withhold context; let the reader fall in before they understand.
- **For an essayistic frame,** open on the core paradox/thesis stated plainly as fact ("The first thing you need to understand is that an infinite world can fit in a finite space").
- **Close** per core move #7: short, reframing, lapidary; ring composition; the diminished concrete image or coined epithet last. If there's a frame (footnotes, apparatus), give it the true last word and let it decline to resolve.
- **Withhold the title phrase** from the opening; plant it once, cryptically, then detonate it at the recognition scene so the title earns its meaning retroactively.

## Rhetorical devices (quick reference)

- **Triads / rule-of-three** for force and completeness.
- **Anaphora** — successive clauses from one opener — for litany and crescendo; cap with a three-beat.
- **Antithesis / balanced pairs** — the prose thinks in opposed alternatives ("if not X, then Y; if X, then Z").
- **Asyndeton** (drop conjunctions) for urgency; **polysyndeton** (pile "and") for ritual/accumulation.
- **Epizeuxis** — immediate word-doubling ("Think, think, think") for strain or a flinch.
- **Epistrophe / refrain** — repeat a charged word (optionally italicized) within a few lines to drive the theme.
- **Reveal-by-relabeling** — name a thing neutrally, then rename it in escalating terms on their own lines:
  > Two bodies. A man and a woman.
  >
  > A father and a mother.
- **Discourse signposts** ("Now then —", "Tellingly,", "Either way —", "Moreover") to glue an essayistic narrator's leaps and perform donnish authority.

## Sounding native: the English-fluency pass

The drafts come from a Polish-native writer. Alongside the voice work, fix first-language interference so the prose reads as a native wrote it. Common targets:

- **Articles (a/an/the).** Polish has none, so drafts drop or misplace them, or over-use "the" for abstractions. Set them by English convention; use a bare/definite form deliberately to keep a monumental abstraction stark ("the Absolute").
- **Prepositions and collocations.** Fix calqued prepositions and verb+preposition pairs that don't match English usage.
- **Calqued idioms.** Replace literal-translated Polish idioms with the equivalent English idiom (or recast). If a stock idiom is used, let the narrator own it knowingly.
- **Aspect → tense.** Polish encodes habitual/ongoing/completed in the verb. Render the habitual with "would" + verb or "used to" and frequency adverbs ("every time," "daily"); the ongoing with the progressive; the decisively completed with a crisp simple past.
- **Word order / emphasis.** Polish gets end-focus and fronting for free from case endings. English can't reorder as freely — recover the emphasis with fronting, cleft constructions ("It was X who…"), dashes, or by isolating the stressed word in its own fragment, not by literal inversion.
- **Pronouns.** Polish drops subject pronouns (the verb carries them), so one conjugated word is a whole sentence. English needs the pronoun — keep the one-beat punch with the shortest possible subject-verb ("I spin."), and accept the extra word rather than forcing a bare fragment.
- **Diminutives.** Polish suffixes carry affection/contempt/smallness in one word. Render the *affect* lexically: "little," "tiny," "a measly pebble," "your silly numbers," a pet name.
- **Comma habits.** Polish punctuation rules differ (e.g., commas before every subordinator). Repunctuate to English norms.
- **Archaic/elevated layer.** Where the Polish reached for old morphology (emphatic particles, vocative address), reach for English archaism instead — King-James cadence, "for," "lo," "verily," fronted predicates, formulaic openers ("There once was…") — to mark the elevated register that the modern idiom then undercuts.

Make these fixes invisibly: the goal is prose that never signals it was translated *or* corrected.

## Anti-patterns — do NOT

- **Over-stylize.** The #1 failure. Don't force a fragment, an em-dash, a metaphor, and an aphorism into every paragraph. The stories are disciplined; be disciplined.
- **Turn it purple.** No ornament for ornament's sake. Imagery is load-bearing or it's cut.
- **Invent content.** No new plot, facts, characters, lines, or events. Re-voice only.
- **Flatten the register to one tone.** The collision is the point; don't smooth it away.
- **Impose a single mode** (always-clinical or always-breathless) regardless of the passage.
- **Pile up unearned short sentences** with no long ones to detonate against — that's just choppy, not the rhythm dial.
- **Explain the ending.** The last line reframes; it does not summarize or moralize.
- **Import Polish dialogue dashes** or other L1 conventions into the English.
- **Balloon the length.** Re-voicing tightens more often than it expands.

## Provenance — where these rules came from

Each story contributed; the fingerprint is what they share.

- **Rzeź niewiniątka** — scholarly mock-essay (footnotes, citations) wrapped around a fairy-tale parable; reveal-by-relabeling; under-tell the worst, then twist; planted symbolic objects (mirror-stone, two-faced coin).
- **Kula Borgesa** — Borgesian metaphysical essay-fiction; paradox stated as proof; one master image (sphere/mirror) re-angled; parenthetical self-doubt; coined-epithet ending ("the last blind-god").
- **Cesarz** — deadpan mock-biography; absurd-as-bureaucratic-fact; em-dash narrator asides; extended metaphor (empire over destitution); speculative tech as flat background; diminished final image.
- **Czołem, przyjacielu** — clinical field-note character sketch; habitual/iterative mode; register collision (bureaucratic + manipulative scripted card); a single ironic intrusion as the closing verdict; mood carried by worn objects.
- **Hipotermia** — dying-spacecraft monologue; fragments and single-word sentences; mimetic typographic degradation at the climax; technical-vs-childlike register clash; bathos; ring composition.
- **Król żyraf** — metaphysical noir thriller; the rhythm dial at full stretch (erudition ↔ detonation); the metafictional refrain "convention demanded it"; seeded image-families (coin, mirror, lion/giraffe) fused in the closing image; understatement at the peak.
- **Pomyłka** — theological flash fiction on one controlling metaphor (God-as-author); register clash (archaic ↔ modern); sentence-length as pacing; deflate-after-inflate; isolated italic closing thought.
